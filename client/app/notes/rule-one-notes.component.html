Rule #1 and the Four Ms

A Rule #1 investor looks at stocks as businesses that have a determinable value, and then waits patiently for market fluctuations to bring her that business at a great price.

Knowing you will make money comes from buying a wonderful business at an attractive price.

wonderful implies that the business has Meaning to you - that you understand it enough to want to own the whole thing if you could, that you'd be proud to own it, and that the business reflects your values.

wonderful means that the business meets certain criteria in terms of financial strength and predictability; in particular, it must have a so-called Moat, And, third, it must have good Management.

By "attractive" I mean we can buy the business with a very big Margin of Safety (MOS). To me a nice MOS is buying a dollar of value for fifty cents. So, once I know what a business is worth, I want to buy it for half that price.

If it's predictable, we can determine a value. If it's not a predictable business, it's impossible to know what it's worth.

  1. Find a wonderful business.
  2. Know what it's worth as a business.
  3. Buy it at 50 percent off.
  4. Repeat until very rich.

The price of a thing is not always equal to its value.

Buying stocks as businesses is just like that, except there is no sticker price on the window. We have to figure out what the sticker price is, and then pay less.

In essence, Rule #1 is just about being a good shopper.

All it takes is learning how to determine the values of wonderul companies and waiting for the market to sell you those companies at discounted prices.

The Sticker Price of a business is determined by the kind of surplus cash it can produce for its owners in the future.

"the Four Ms": Meaning, Moat, Management, and Margin of Safety.

  1. Does the business have Meaning to you? you'd be willing to make this business the solo financial support of you family for the next 100 years?
  2. Does the business have a wide Moat? be able to defend itself against attacks by competitors
  3. Does the business have great Management? people running the business are doing so as if it were their family's only means of support for the next 100 years. ...and not going to rip you off for their short-term benefit
  4. Does the business have a big Margin of Safety? know the value of the business and can get it on sale

If you can answer a big unconditional YES to all four of these questions, you'll know if this business is one you want to buy.

sell it later without losing money

There are opportunities to buy wonderful companies at attractive prices - they really do exist - if you're willing to do your homework and say good-bye to mutual funds.

Buy a Business, Not a Stock

The First M question - Does the business have Meaning to you? - implies two other questions: (1) Do you want to own the whole business? and (2) Do you understand it well enough to own all of it?

If we buy business as a business and not as a stock speculation, then it becomes personal. I want it to be personal. I want to be proud of what I own.

select the five or ten companies you like the best. THink of investing your money like planting seeds in the ground.

The 10-10 Rule: I won't own this business for ten minutes unless I'm willing to own it for ten years.

we might buy the business today and sell it in a month and then buy it back three months later and then sell it again a few weeks after that. The fact that we buy it as if we're going to keep it for ten years does not preclude us from buying and selling this business over and over.

How crazy would that be? If his one business is too risky, why would diversifying into five more businesses make the first one less risky?

We have the luxury of waiting patiently until we can find wonderful companies at attractive prices...

Meaning, in the Rule #1 sense, is just about inderstanding what a company does and how it does it - enought to be happy to put your name on the business and expect to keep it there for the next hundred yeards or so.

Identify a Moat

Finding a business with a wide Moat is key to finding a sucessful business to own, because a business with a wide Moat is much more predictable for the next 20 years than a business

durable competitive advantage that protects it from attack, like a moat protects a castle

If an indeustry looks as if it might be very easey to get into, there probably isn't a Moat. On the other hand, if it looks as though i might be really hard to get into and be successful, you'll probably find some wide-Moat businesses.

Commodity businesses don't have Moats. If you own a commodity business, you have exactly zero protectionfrom commpetitors.

What that wide Moat means to a business owner is that the business is very hard to compete against.

You can't compete with Coke by lowering your price, and that's also a sign of a wide Moat.

What's even more spectacular about businesses with wide Moats is they can keep u with inflation. In other words, they can raise prices as their costs go up.

Wal-Mart ha a Moat based on price. Pfizer gets its Moat from patents on drugs. Intel gets its Moat from its long experience in building chips. PG&E protects itself by government law.

That's the purpose of the Moat around the castle: protection from inflation and competition.

Rule #1 companies - can do that because they have some kind of monopolistic position in the market.

Moats can be created by trade secrets that keep other businesses from copying them.

It doesn't have to be a "monopoly" in a classic sense (meaning it has exclusive control over a particular area of the market), but is should be a company that is well known for being number one or number two in its industry.

The five moats

Brand
a product you're willing to pay more for because you trust it e.g. Coke, Gillette, Disney, McDonald's, Pepsi, Nike, Budweiser, Harley-Davidson
Secret
a business that has a patent or trade secret that makes direct competition illegal or very difficult e.g. Pfizer, 3M, Intel
Toll
a business with exclusive control of a market - giving it the ability to collect a "toll" form anyone needing that service or product e.g. media companies, utilites , ad agencies
Switching
a business that's so much a part of your life that switching isn't worth the trouble e.g. ADR Paychex, H&R Block, Microsoft
Price
a business that can price products so low no one can compete e.g. Wall-Mart, Costco, Bed Bath & Beyond, Home Depot, Target

AS business buyers, we must understand that there are many forces affecting stock prices - forces that often have nothing to do with the quality of the company or the width of its Moat.

Understand tha tmOat tells us whether a company is likely to be around in 20 years, not what price we should be paying for it right now. Wide-Moat businesses are often massively overpriced and, as a result can drop in price like there is no tomorrow.

Our job is to find businesses with obviously wide Moats, buy them when the market has mispriced them too low, then sell them when the market has mispriced them too high.

Rule #1 investors, however, take the guesswork out of identifying a Moat by looking at five numbers in particular.

If business has at least one of the Five Moats, it'll show up in the Big Five numbers. These numbers are proof of the existence of a Moat.

Here are the Big Five:

  1. Return on Investment Capital (ROIC)
  2. Sales growth rate
  3. Earning per Share (EPS) growth rate
  4. Equity, or Book Value per Share (BVPS), growth rate
  5. Free Cash Flow (FCF or Cash) growth rate

All of the big Five should be equal to or greater than 10 percent per year of the last 10 years.

Calculate the Big Five

PRIORITY OF GROWTH RATES

Not all four growth rate vear the same weight, or share the same level of importance. Alghough they all should be above our 10-percent minimum, here's their order of importance, starting with the most important:

  1. Equity growth
  2. EPS growth
  3. Sales (or gross profit) growth
  4. Cash flow growth

This means that when you have to refer to growth rates or derive other estimations form them, which w'll be doing shortly, you should focus on equity and EPS growth first, and then sales and cash flow. Equity and EPS growth say a lot about the future of a company, and how certain we can be in predicting that future, which is a cornerstone to Rule #1.

Net operating profit after tax is often shortened to NOPAT. To get NOPAT, start with net income from operations, but add back in any interest and depreciation expenses.
